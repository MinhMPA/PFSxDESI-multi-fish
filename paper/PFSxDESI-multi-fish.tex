\keywords{galaxy surveys, redshift surveys, galaxy clustering, power spectrum, cosmological parameters from LSS, neutrino masses from cosmology}

\abstract{%
Marginalizing over roughly 12 effective-field-theory (EFT) nuisance
parameters per tracer per redshift bin is the dominant systematic cost
in full-shape galaxy power spectrum analyses.
Simulation-based priors (SBP) can tighten these parameters but rely on
N-body simulations and halo-occupation-distribution (HOD) models.
We propose multi-survey priors: a data-driven alternative
that calibrates EFT parameters from the multi-tracer analysis of
overlapping spectroscopic surveys.
%
In the \(\sim\!1{,}200\;\text{deg}^2\) Prime Focus Spectrograph (PFS)--Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI) overlap at
\(0.6<z<1.6\), up to 4 tracers (PFS-ELG, DESI-ELG, DESI-LRG,
DESI-QSO) and 10 cross-spectra per redshift bin provide
model-independent constraints on the DESI nuisance parameters.
Cross-spectra between different galaxy populations carry zero
stochastic contribution, cleanly separating shot noise from signal.
Exporting the calibrated priors to the full
\(14{,}000\;\text{deg}^2\) DESI footprint of the DESI LRG1--3, ELG1--2 and
QSO samples improves \(\sigma(f\se)\) by 8\% (7--25\% per sample) and
\(\sigma(\Mnu)\) by 54\% (46--71\% per sample) at
\(\kmax=0.20\iMpch\).
A parameter importance decomposition reveals that the dominant driver
is the calibration of the \(b_1\se\) prior---constrained from a flat prior
to \(\sigma\approx 0.17\) by the multi-tracer Fisher---which
accounts for \(\sim\!70\%\) of the \(f\se\) gain and
\(\sim\!97\%\) of the \(\Mnu\) gain by breaking the
\(b_1\sigma_8\)--\(f\se\) degeneracy intrinsic to single-tracer analyses.
Multi-survey prior calibration parallels the SBP approach, which tightens \(b_1\)
through HOD model assumptions; multi-survey priors provide a
model-independent consistency check on these assumptions and
generalize to arbitrary combinations of overlapping spectroscopic surveys. Code is available at this repository \github{https://github.com/MinhMPA/PFSxDESI-multi-fish}.
}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------

Full-shape analyses of the galaxy power
spectrum, built on the effective field theory of large-scale
structure
(EFTofLSS)~\cite{Baumann:2010tm,Carrasco:2012cv,Carroll:2013oxa,Senatore:2014eva}, have now become the new standard in analyses of galaxy clustering in spectroscopic surveys.
From their first applications on the Sloan Digital Sky Survey BOSS galaxy samples~\citep[e.g.,][]{dAmico:2019fhj,Ivanov:2019pdj}, full-shape EFT analyses have improved LSS constraints on, for example, neutrino masses~\cite{Colas:2019ret,Ivanov:2019hqk,Chudaykin:2025lww}, primordial
non-Gaussianity~\cite{Cabass:2022wjy,DAmico:2022gki,Chudaykin:2025vdh}, early dark energy~\cite{Ivanov:2020ril,DAmico:2020ods,Smith:2020rxx}, clustering dark energy~\cite{DAmico:2020tty}, interacting dark energy~\cite{Tsedrik:2025cwc}, ultra-light axions~\cite{Rogers:2023ezo} and dark-sector
interactions~\cite{He:2023oke,He:2025npy}.
More recently, the DESI collaboration~\cite{DESI:2024jxi,DESI:2024hhd,DESI:2025wzd} and independent
reanalyses~\cite{Chudaykin:2025aux,Chudaykin:2025lww,Chudaykin:2025vdh,Ivanov:2026dvl,Chudaykin:2026nls} applied this framework to DESI~DR1 data, reporting evidence for dynamical dark energy and---when combined with CMB data---a preference for the normal neutrino mass hierarchy over the inverted hierarchy. Sharpening \(\sigma(\Mnu)\) from galaxy clustering is therefore a pressing goal, as the neutrino mass constraint is currently the most prior-sensitive quantity in EFT full-shape analyses.

At next-to-leading (i.e.\ one-loop) order, the EFT framework
parameterizes our ignorance of small-scale physics through
roughly 12 nuisance parameters per tracer per redshift
bin~\cite{Chudaykin:2020aoj,Desjacques:2016bnm}---bias coefficients,
counterterms, and stochastic amplitudes (\cref{tab:eft_params}).
Marginalizing over these parameters with conservative Gaussian priors
degrades \(\sigma(f\se)\) by a factor of \(\sim\!2.4\) and
\(\sigma(\Mnu)\) by \(\sim\!6.4\) relative to perfect nuisance
knowledge for a single DESI-ELG sample~\cite{Chudaykin:2024wlw};
the degradation grows to \(\sim\!4.6\times\) and \(\sim\!10\times\)
when 6 DR2 samples are combined (\cref{sec:conclusions}), illustrating
the increasing cost of nuisance-parameter marginalization in joint
multi-sample (not multi-tracer) analyses.

Tightening the nuisance priors is therefore a direct route to sharper
cosmological constraints. All existing approaches to informative
priors are simulation-based: HOD mock catalogs are first generated
from N-body simulations, the EFT parameters are then measured from the
mocks, either at the power-spectrum level or at the field
level, and the resulting marginal distributions of EFT parameters are used as priors in the EFT full-shape analyses~\cite{Ivanov:2024hgq,Ivanov:2024xgb,Zhang:2024thl}. Zhang et
al.~\cite{DESI:2025wzd} applied power-spectrum-level priors to
DESI DR1, improving \(\sigma(\se)\) by \(\sim\!23\%\); Chudaykin,
Ivanov \& Philcox~\cite{Chudaykin:2026nls} achieved
\(\sim\!50\%\) with field-level priors. These gains are
substantial, but the simulation-based priors (SBP) are only as accurate as the HOD model used to produce them~\cite{Akitsu:2024lyt}. Notably, the SBP analyses
shift \(\se\) downward by \(\sim\!2\sigma\) relative to conservative
priors~\cite{Chudaykin:2026nls}, deepening the tension with CMB
measurements of \(S_8\). Whether this shift reflects new physics or
systematic biases absorbed from the HOD modeling remains an open
question---one that an independent, data-driven calibration of the
same parameters could help resolve.

Meanwhile, multi-tracer Fisher forecasts have shown that jointly fitting multiple galaxy populations can break parameter degeneracies beyond what single-tracer analyses can achieve~\cite{Alarcon:2016bkr,Mergulhao:2021kip,Mergulhao:2023zso,Rubira:2025scu,Qin:2025nkk}.
Zhao et al.~\cite{Zhao:2024eboss} demonstrated this with eBOSS DR16 data by jointly analyzing LRGs and ELGs within a single survey, improving \(\sigma(\se)\) by 18--27\% depending on the treatment of stochastic terms in the LRG--ELG cross spectrum. This demonstrates the statistical value of multi-tracer information, but not the calibration of survey-independent EFT priors: same-survey analyses share observational systematics, and even for disjoint tracer catalogs with no shared-object shot noise, residual cross-stochasticity remains a modeling assumption.

In this paper we demonstrate that EFT nuisance parameters can be
calibrated directly from observational data, without HOD modeling.
We propose \emph{multi-survey priors}---a data-driven alternative to
SBP that exploits the overlap volume between two
independent spectroscopic surveys. When PFS and DESI observe the same
patch of sky, cross-spectra between disjoint galaxy populations
contain no shared-object shot noise by construction; a multi-tracer
Fisher analysis of these auto- and cross-spectra isolates the
stochastic nuisance parameters model-independently. The calibrated priors are then exported to the full
\(14{,}000\;\text{deg}^2\) DESI footprint, tightening cosmological
constraints without any simulation input.

The \(\sim\!1{,}200\;\text{deg}^2\) overlap of the Subaru Prime Focus
Spectrograph (PFS) ELG survey~\cite{Takada:2014pfs} with 3 DESI
tracers---ELG, luminous red galaxies (LRG), and quasars
(QSO)~\cite{DESI:2016fyo,DESI:2025zgx}---at \(0.6<z<1.6\)
provides a concrete realization. With up to 4 tracers and 10 cross-spectra per redshift bin, the overlap volume functions as a calibration laboratory for the EFT parameters that limit the full DESI analysis. The weaker finger-of-god (FoG) contamination expected in PFS samples motivates an asymmetric scale cut, \(\kmax^{\PFS}>\kmax^{\DESI}\)~\cite{Rubira:2025scu}\footnote{Data-driven FoG cleaning methods~\citep[e.g.,][]{BaleatoLizancos:2025wdg} offer a complementary route to extending the usable \(k\)-range.}.

This paper is organized as follows. \Cref{sec:method} describes the
multi-survey prior mechanism. \Cref{sec:fisher} details the Fisher
forecast setup, including the PFS and DESI survey specifications.
\Cref{sec:results} presents the calibrated priors,
the full-area DESI constraints, the calibration efficiency, and
sensitivity tests. We discuss implications in \cref{sec:discussion}
and conclude in \cref{sec:conclusions}.

\section{Multi-survey priors}
\label{sec:method}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Marginalization cost}
\label{sec:marg_cost}

The one-loop EFTofLSS model for the galaxy power spectrum multipoles
\(P_\ell(k)\) involves roughly 12 nuisance parameters per tracer per
redshift bin, summarized in \cref{tab:eft_params}: 3 bias parameters
(\(b_1\se\), \(b_2\se^2\), \(b_{\G_2}\se^2\)) that enter the Fisher
matrix as explicit rows; the cubic tidal bias \(b_{\Gamma_3}\); 5
counterterms (\(c_0\), \(c_2\), \(c_4\), \(\ctilde\), \(c_1\)) that
absorb UV sensitivity of the loop integrals; and 3 stochastic
amplitudes (\(\Pshot\), \(a_0\), \(a_2\)) that parameterize departures
from Poisson shot noise.

In a real Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) analysis, the counterterms and stochastic amplitudes
enter the model linearly or quadratically and are marginalized
analytically~\cite{Chudaykin:2020aoj}. Their Gaussian priors therefore
propagate directly into the marginalized posterior on cosmological
parameters; broader nuisance priors therefore mean weaker cosmological
constraints, regardless of the sampler used.

The cost is substantial. As noted in the Introduction,
tightening nuisance priors from conservative to HOD-informed
improves \(\sigma(\se)\) by 23--50\% depending on whether
power-spectrum-level or field-level SBPs are
used~\cite{DESI:2025wzd,Chudaykin:2026nls}. The nuisance prior
is the dominant bottleneck. The question is how to tighten it
without relying on simulation assumptions.


\subsection{Cross-power constraints on nuisance parameters}
\label{sec:cross_power}

The multi-tracer analysis of overlapping surveys provides exactly
this---it isolates bias and stochastic parameters from the
cross-spectrum between independent galaxy populations.
The approach exploits the different stochastic
properties of distinct galaxy populations~\citep[e.g.,][]{Seljak:2008xr,McDonald:2009cvfree,Mergulhao:2021kip,Mergulhao:2023zso,Rubira:2025scu}.
The auto-power spectrum of tracer \(X\) at multipole \(\ell\) can be written schematically as
\be
\label{eqn:auto}
P_\ell^{XX}(k)
= P_\ell^{XX,\mathrm{det}}(k;\, b_1^X, b_2^X, \ldots, c_0^X, \ctilde^X)
+ \frac{1}{\nbar_X}\!\left(
  1 + \Pshot^X + a_0^X \frac{k^2}{\knl^2} + \ldots
\right)\!,
\ee
where the first term is the deterministic (signal) contribution from
tree-level and one-loop perturbation theory---parameterized by the
bias coefficients \(b_1, b_2, b_{G_2}\) that encode the galaxy--matter
connection and the counterterms \(c_0, c_2, \ctilde\) that absorb
unresolved small-scale physics---and the second is the stochastic contribution---Poisson shot noise (\(1/\nbar\)) plus
EFT corrections (\(\Pshot\), \(a_0\), \(a_2\)) that capture
departures from Poissonian discreteness.

The cross-power spectrum between tracers \(X\) and \(Y\) is
\be
\label{eqn:cross}
P_\ell^{XY}(k)
= P_\ell^{XY,\mathrm{det}}(k)
+ P_{\rm shot}^{XY}\,,
\ee
where the cross-stochastic term \(P_{\rm shot}^{XY}\) depends on
whether the two catalogs share galaxies. For different galaxy populations---PFS-ELG\(\,\times\,\)%
DESI-LRG or PFS-ELG\(\,\times\,\)DESI-QSO---the catalogs have zero
overlap and the cross-stochastic vanishes exactly:
\(\delta_{XY}^{\rm shared}=0\). These cross-spectra provide a clean,
model-independent measurement of the deterministic signal, free of
any shot-noise contamination.

For similar populations---PFS-ELG\(\,\times\,\)DESI-ELG---a
fraction of galaxies appear in both catalogs (both surveys target
[OII] emitters, and PFS goes \(\sim\!1\;\text{mag}\) deeper in
[OII] flux). The cross-shot noise depends on both number densities:
\be
\label{eqn:cross_shot}
P_{\rm shot}^{\PFS\times\DESI\text{-ELG}}
= \frac{n_{\rm shared}}{\nbar_{\PFS}\,\nbar_{\DESI\text{-ELG}}}
= \frac{f_{\rm shared}}{\nbar_{\DESI\text{-ELG}}}\,,
\ee
where \(n_{\rm shared}\) is the number density of galaxies
appearing in both catalogs and
\(f_{\rm shared}\equiv n_{\rm shared}/\nbar_{\PFS}\) is
the fraction of PFS-ELGs shared with DESI.
This interpolates smoothly between zero (\(f_{\rm shared}=0\),
fully independent catalogs) and \(1/\nbar_{\DESI\text{-ELG}}\)
(\(f_{\rm shared}=1\), all PFS-ELGs also in DESI).
We adopt a fiducial \(f_{\rm shared}=0.045\), following the observing strategy of the PFS Cosmology Survey~\cite{PFS-CO-target:inprep}, and verify in
\cref{sec:results_sensitivity} that the impact on cosmological
constraints is negligible (\(\sigma(f\se)\) varies by
\({<}\,\pm 1\) percentage point across
\(f_{\rm shared}\in[0,1]\)).

The key observation is that jointly fitting the auto-spectra
\(P_\ell^{XX}\), \(P_\ell^{YY}\) and the cross-spectrum
\(P_\ell^{XY}\) at each \(k\) over-determines the system. The
deterministic signal is shared (up to bias ratios), while the
stochastic terms enter only the auto spectrum (or are quantifiably
suppressed in the cross spectrum). The multi-tracer combination therefore
isolates the constant shot-noise departure \(\Pshot\), which
enters the monopole as a \(k\)-independent offset, far more tightly
than any single tracer can. The \(k\)-dependent stochastic terms
\(a_0\) and \(a_2\), by contrast, are degenerate with the leading
counterterms \(c_0\) and \(c_2\) (both scale as \(k^2\)) and
benefit less from the cross spectrum~\cite{Zhao:2024eboss}. The one-loop bias structure additionally constrains \(b_{\G_2}\) through its distinct \(\mu\)-dependence in the loop integrals~\cite{Mergulhao:2021kip,Mergulhao:2023zso}.


\subsection{Asymmetric scale cuts}
\label{sec:asymmetric_kmax}

PFS-ELGs reside in host halos of relatively lower mass than those of DESI-ELGs or DESI-LRGs~\cite{PFS-CO-target:inprep} and consequently have lower virial velocity dispersion, $\sigma_v$. In the EFT framework, this translates into a smaller FoG counterterm, \(\ctilde^{\PFS} = \ctilde^{\DESI\text{-ELG}} \times \rsv^4\), where
\(\rsv \equiv \sigma_{v,\PFS}/\sigma_{v,\DESI}\).

A smaller \(\ctilde\) extends the perturbative range of
validity~\cite{Rubira:2025scu}, allowing the one-loop EFT model
to remain valid to higher \(k\). Since
\(\kmax \propto \ctilde^{-1/4} \propto \rsv^{-1}\),
\be
\label{eqn:kmax_asym}
\kmax^{\PFS} = \kmax^{\DESI} \times \rsv^{-1}\,.
\ee
For our fiducial \(\rsv=0.75\), this gives
\(\kmax^{\PFS}\approx 0.27\iMpch\) when \(\kmax^{\DESI}=0.20\iMpch\).

We implement this by splitting the Fisher matrix sum into two
\(k\)-ranges~\cite{Rubira:2025scu}. At
\(k \leq \kmax^{\DESI}\), all \(N_t(N_t{+}1)/2\) spectra contribute
(up to 10 spectra for 4 tracers). At
\(\kmax^{\DESI} < k \leq \kmax^{\PFS}\), the DESI auto-spectra are
excluded and only the PFS auto and the PFS\(\times\)DESI cross-spectra
remain. The total Fisher matrix is given by
\be
\label{eqn:fisher_asym}
F_{\rm overlap}
= \sum_{k\,\leq\,\kmax^{\DESI}}
  \mathbf{D}^T \mathbf{C}^{-1} \mathbf{D}\,\Delta k
+ \sum_{\kmax^{\DESI}\,<\,k\,\leq\,\kmax^{\PFS}}
  \mathbf{D}_{\rm red}^T \mathbf{C}_{\rm red}^{-1} \mathbf{D}_{\rm red}\,\Delta k\,,
\ee
where \(\mathbf{D}\) is the derivative matrix of the full observable
vector and \(\mathbf{D}_{\rm red}\), \(\mathbf{C}_{\rm red}\) are
the reduced versions excluding the DESI auto-spectrum rows and columns.


\subsection{Prior export mechanism}
\label{sec:prior_export}

From the overlap multi-tracer Fisher \(F_{\rm overlap}\) (\cref{eqn:fisher_asym}), we extract calibrated priors for each DESI nuisance parameter in three steps:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Regularize the Fisher matrix: \(F_{\rm reg} = F_{\rm overlap} + F_{\rm broad}\),
  where \(F_{\rm broad}\) is diagonal with entries \(1/\sigma_{\rm broad}^2\)
  for each nuisance parameter (from \cref{tab:eft_params}) and weak
  cosmological priors (\(\sigma(f\se)_{\rm prior}=10\),
  \(\sigma(\Om)_{\rm prior}=1\), \(\sigma(\Mnu)_{\rm prior}=5\;\text{eV}\)).
\item Invert the Fisher matrix: \(C = F_{\rm reg}^{-1}\).
\item Extract diagonal elements: \(\sigma_{\rm cal}(\theta_i) = \sqrt{C_{ii}}\) for each DESI nuisance parameter \(\theta_i\).
\end{enumerate}

The calibrated \(\sigma_{\rm cal}\) values are then used as Gaussian
priors in the full-area DESI Fisher forecast over
\(14{,}000\;\text{deg}^2\). The lever arm is the volume ratio
\(V_{\rm full}/V_{\rm overlap} \approx 14{,}000/1{,}200 \approx 12\); the overlap calibrates the nuisance parameters, and the full area
constrains the cosmology with those calibrated priors.

In a real data analysis, the procedure generalizes naturally.
Step~1 becomes a full MCMC on the multi-tracer likelihood in the
overlap volume, yielding the joint posterior on all cosmological
and nuisance parameters. Step~2 marginalizes this posterior over
cosmological parameters and other tracers' nuisance parameters to
obtain the marginal posterior for each DESI nuisance parameter.
Step~3 uses these marginal posteriors, or Gaussian fits to
them, as priors in the full-area DESI MCMC. The Fisher
extraction above is the Gaussian limit of this procedure;
inverting \(F_{\rm reg}\) gives the Gaussian posterior covariance,
and extracting the diagonal is equivalent to marginalization under
the Gaussian assumption.

Formally, the full-area Fisher in Step~2 includes the
\(1{,}200\;\text{deg}^2\) overlap used in Step~1, so the
cosmological information in the overlap is counted twice---once
implicitly through the calibrated priors and once explicitly in
the full-area sum. We have verified that this double-counting is
negligible. Repeating the six-sample forecast with the overlap
excluded from Step~2 (\(12{,}800\;\text{deg}^2\) instead of
\(14{,}000\;\text{deg}^2\)) changes the improvement on
\(\sigma(f\se)\) from 8.2\% to 8.5\% and on \(\sigma(\Mnu)\) from
53.7\% to 54.6\%---a shift of \({<}\,1\) percentage point,
well within the precision of the forecast.


\subsection{Comparison with simulation-based priors}
\label{sec:sbp_comparison}

SBPs~\cite{Ivanov:2024hgq,Zhang:2024thl,Chudaykin:2026nls} encode
the full joint distribution of EFT parameters from HOD mocks and
achieve tighter constraints than diagonal Gaussian priors, but their
accuracy is bounded by the fidelity of the galaxy--halo connection
model. Cross-calibration trades this model dependence for a volume
limitation: the overlap constrains \(b_1\), \(\Pshot\), and
\(b_{\G_2}\) model-independently, while SBPs tighten the counterterms
and \(k\)-dependent stochasticity that the overlap volume cannot
reach. The two are complementary---and because they constrain the
same parameters through independent mechanisms, they serve as
mutual consistency checks (\cref{sec:discussion}).
The power-spectrum-level SBP of Zhang et al.~\cite{DESI:2025wzd}
(\(\sim\!23\%\) on \(\sigma(\se)\)) is the fair comparison for our
work; the field-level SBP of Chudaykin et
al.~\cite{Chudaykin:2026nls} (\(\sim\!50\%\)) breaks degeneracies
inaccessible to two-point statistics.

\section{Fisher forecast setup}
\label{sec:fisher}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------

To forecast the constraints from the PFS--DESI overlap, we construct
multi-tracer and single-tracer Fisher matrices using the one-loop
EFTofLSS. We describe the survey specifications, the EFT
parameterization, the covariance and derivative computation, and the
analysis scenarios below.


\subsection{Survey specifications}
\label{sec:surveys}

We consider the overlap of PFS-ELG with 3 DESI tracer samples in
the shared \(\sim\!1{,}200\;\text{deg}^2\) footprint.

\paragraph{DESI.}
The Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument~\cite{DESI:2016fyo} covers
\(14{,}000\;\text{deg}^2\) with 3 galaxy samples relevant to this
analysis. The ELG sample~\cite{DESI:2025zgx} spans
\(0.8<z<1.6\) with peak \(\nbar\sim 10^{-3}\Mpchcubed\) and bias
\(b_1(z) = 0.84/D(z)\). The LRG sample covers \(0.4<z<1.1\) with
\(\nbar\sim 5\times 10^{-4}\Mpchcubed\) and \(b_1(z)=1.7/D(z)\)---the
highest bias of the 3, providing strong contrast with PFS. The QSO
sample extends to \(z\sim 2.1\) but has low density
(\(\nbar\sim 3\times 10^{-5}\Mpchcubed\)) and \(b_1(z)=1.2/D(z)\).

\paragraph{PFS.}
The Subaru Prime Focus Spectrograph~\cite{Takada:2014pfs} will survey
\(\sim\!1{,}200\;\text{deg}^2\) with an ELG sample spanning
\(0.6<z<2.4\), reaching \(\sim\!1\;\text{mag}\) deeper in [OII] flux
than DESI and achieving peak \(\nbar\sim 9\times 10^{-4}\Mpchcubed\) at
\(z\sim 1\). Here, we assume the linear bias to be \(b_1(z)=0.9+0.4z\)~\cite{Orsi:2010obl}.

\paragraph{Overlap geometry.}
The joint redshift coverage determines which tracers overlap in
each bin. PFS-ELG (\(0.6<z<2.4\)) overlaps with DESI-LRG
(\(0.4<z<1.1\)) at \(0.6<z<1.1\), with DESI-ELG
(\(0.8<z<1.6\)) at \(0.8<z<1.6\), and with DESI-QSO
(\(0.8<z<2.1\)) at \(0.8<z<1.6\) (we truncate at
\(z=1.6\) where DESI-ELG ends).
We rebin the overlap into 5 matched redshift bins:
\(z=[0.6,\,0.8]\), \([0.8,\,1.0]\), \([1.0,\,1.2]\),
\([1.2,\,1.4]\), and \([1.4,\,1.6]\).
Number densities are volume-weighted averages from
fine-binned \(n(z)\) tables (\(\Delta z=0.01\)); bias parameters are evaluated
at the volume-weighted effective redshift.
\Cref{tab:surveys} summarizes the survey properties per bin. At

\subsection{PFS-ELG EFT fiducials}
\label{sec:pfs_fiducials}

Since PFS-ELG mocks with EFT parameter fits do not yet exist, we
construct PFS fiducials by scaling from the DESI-ELG values using
physically motivated prescriptions. The FoG counterterm scales with
the squared virial velocity:
\(\ctilde^{\PFS}=\ctilde^{\DESI\text{-ELG}}\times \rsv^4\), where
\(\rsv\equiv \sigma_{v,\PFS}/\sigma_{v,\DESI}\). We adopt a fiducial
\(\rsv=0.75\) (PFS selects fainter [OII] emitters in lower-mass halos~\cite{PFS-CO-target:inprep})
and test the range \(\rsv\in[0.5,\,1.0]\) in \cref{sec:results_sensitivity}.
The leading counterterms (\(c_0\), \(c_2\), \(c_4\)) scale with the
bias ratio \(b_1^{\PFS}/b_1^{\DESI}\); the nonlinear biases \(b_2\)
and \(b_{\G_2}\) are set from the Lazeyras et al.\
\cite{Lazeyras:2015lgp} co-evolution relations.

For the DESI-LRG and DESI-QSO samples, we set \(\ctilde\) using the
virial velocity dispersions measured by Zhang et al.\
\cite{DESI:2025wzd} (their Tab.~1): \(\sigma_v^{\rm LRG}=6.20\Mpch\),
\(\sigma_v^{\rm QSO}=5.68\Mpch\), \(\sigma_v^{\rm ELG}=3.11\Mpch\).
Scaling from the DESI-ELG fiducial via
\(\ctilde^X=\ctilde^{\rm ELG}\times(\sigma_v^X/\sigma_v^{\rm ELG})^4\)
gives \(\ctilde^{\rm LRG}\approx 6{,}300\;[\Mpch]^4\) and
\(\ctilde^{\rm QSO}\approx 4{,}500\;[\Mpch]^4\), an order of
magnitude larger than the ELG value. The leading counterterm
\(c_2\) scales with the bias ratio \(b_1^X/b_1^{\rm ELG}\).


\subsection{Multi-tracer Fisher matrix}
\label{sec:mt_fisher}

The observables are power spectrum multipoles
\(P_\ell(k)\) for \(\ell=0,2,4\)~\cite{Ivanov:2019pdj,dAmico:2019fhj},
computed for each auto- and cross-spectrum pair. At each wavenumber \(k\), the observable vector has \(3 N_{\rm spec}\) elements, where \(N_{\rm spec}=N_t(N_t{+}1)/2\) is the number of auto- and cross-spectrum pairs---up to 30 elements for 4 tracers.

The Gaussian covariance at each \(k\) is
\be
\Cov\!\left[P_\ell^{XY},\, P_{\ell'}^{WZ}\right]\!(k)
= \frac{(2\ell{+}1)(2\ell'{+}1)}{2 N_{\rm modes}(k)}
  \int_{-1}^{1}\!\dd\mu\; L_\ell(\mu)\, L_{\ell'}(\mu)\,
  \bigl[P^{XW} P^{YZ}
  + P^{XZ} P^{YW}\bigr],
\ee
where \(N_{\rm modes}(k)=k^2\Delta k\, V/(2\pi^2)\) and
\(P^{XY}(k,\mu)\) includes the one-loop signal, the Poisson
shot noise \(\delta_{XY}/\nbar_X\), and the cross-shot noise
\(P_{\rm shot}^{XY}\) from \cref{eqn:cross_shot}.
The \(\mu\)-integrals are evaluated with 20-point Gauss--Legendre
quadrature. The signal contribution is computed from the one-loop
EFTofLSS model~\cite{Chudaykin:2020aoj} using the FFTLog
decomposition~\cite{Simonovic:2017mhp} implemented in
\texttt{ps\_1loop\_jax}%
\footnote{\url{https://github.com/archaeo-pteryx/ps_1loop_jax}}~\cite{Kobayashi:2026inprep},
ensuring consistency between the covariance
and the derivative evaluation.

Derivatives \(\partial P_\ell/\partial\theta\) with respect to all
nuisance and cosmological parameters are computed via JAX
forward-mode automatic differentiation.
For nuisance parameters, the autodiff traces through the one-loop
\texttt{ps\_1loop\_jax} model; for \(\Om\) and \(\Mnu\), it traces
end-to-end through the
\texttt{cosmopower-jax}~\cite{Piras:2023aub}%
\footnote{\url{https://github.com/dpiras/cosmopower-jax}}
emulator with the \(\nu\Lambda\)CDM-trained
networks of Jense et al.~\cite{Jense:2024llt}%
\footnote{\url{https://github.com/cosmopower-organization/jense_2024_emulators}},
the growth rate, and the one-loop model.
This provides exact derivatives for
all 15 parameters without step-size tuning; we validate against
adaptive finite-difference numerical derivatives in
\cref{app:derivatives}.


\subsection{Analysis scenarios and benchmarks}
\label{sec:scenarios}

The calibrated priors from \cref{sec:prior_export} are applied to the
full-area DESI-ELG Fisher forecast over \(14{,}000\;\text{deg}^2\).
All scenarios use the same \(\kmax=0.20\iMpch\) for the full-area
analysis---the EFT convergence for DESI-ELG is set by the DESI
\(\ctilde\), which is independent of the priors used. The improvement
comes entirely from tighter nuisance priors, not from extending the
\(k\)-range. We evaluate three scenarios:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Broad}: conservative Gaussian priors from
  \cref{tab:eft_params}. Baseline.
\item \textbf{Calibrated}: multi-survey priors from the overlap
  Fisher (\cref{sec:prior_export}).
\item \textbf{Fixed nuisance}: nuisance parameters fixed to
  fiducial values. Theoretical ceiling.
\end{itemize}
We overlay horizontal reference lines at the published improvement
ratios from the PS-level SBP of Zhang et al.\ \cite{DESI:2025wzd}
(23\% on \(\sigma(\se)\)) and the field-level SBP of Chudaykin et al.\
\cite{Chudaykin:2026nls} (50\% on \(\sigma(\se)\)).


\subsection{Transferability assumption}
\label{sec:transferability}

The calibration transfers from overlap to full area if and only if
DESI-ELG in the overlap has the same HOD---and therefore the same
EFT parameters---as DESI-ELG outside the overlap. Since the DESI target selection
is spatially uniform by construction, this holds to the extent that
imaging systematics do not induce spatial variation in the ELG
population; the DESI imaging-systematics pipeline is designed to
control this~\cite{DESI:2025zgx}. We state this as an explicit
assumption and note that a violation would manifest as a detectable
mismatch between the calibrated and in-situ posteriors.

\subsection{Calibrated priors}
\label{sec:results_priors}

\Cref{fig:calibrated_vs_broad} compares the calibrated prior
widths with the conservative broad
priors~\cite{Chudaykin:2025lww,DESI:2024jxi} for all DESI-ELG
nuisance parameters. Three parameters show significant tightening.
The linear bias \(b_1\se\), which carries a flat prior in the
broad analysis, is constrained to
\(\sigma(b_1\se)\approx 0.17\text{--}0.27\) by the multi-tracer
Fisher---a qualitative change from zero to finite prior information.
The cross-spectra constrain the linear bias ratios
\(b_1^X/b_1^Y\) between
tracers~\cite{Seljak:2008xr,McDonald:2009cvfree}, and the
\(\mu\)-dependent RSD structure across \(\ell=0,2,4\) then breaks
the degeneracy between the individual \(b_1\se\) values and
\(f\se\).
The shot-noise departure \(\Pshot\) tightens
by nearly an order of magnitude (from \(\sigma=1\) to
\(\sigma\approx 0.1\text{--}0.4\)), reflecting the clean separation
enabled by the zero-stochastic cross-spectrum. The tidal bias
\(b_{\G_2}\se^2\) tightens by \(3\text{--}6\times\) (from \(\sigma=5\)
to \(\sigma\approx 0.8\text{--}1.8\)), driven by its distinct
\(\mu\)-dependence in the one-loop power spectrum.
As we show in \cref{sec:discussion}, the \(b_1\se\) calibration
is the dominant driver of the cosmological improvement, because
\(\Mnu\) is nearly perfectly degenerate with \(b_1\) in the
single-tracer Fisher (\cref{tab:importance}).
The \(k\)-dependent stochastic terms \(a_0\) and \(a_2\) remain
prior-dominated (96--99\% of broad)---they scale as \(k^2\) and are
degenerate with the counterterms \(c_0\) and \(c_2\). The FoG
counterterm \(\ctilde\) tightens to 87\% of broad at low \(z\),
reflecting the large \(\ctilde\) contrast between tracers
(\(\ctilde^{\rm ELG}=400\) vs \(\ctilde^{\rm LRG}\approx 6{,}300\)).
The remaining counterterms (\(c_0\), \(c_2\), \(c_4\)) tighten only
marginally---from \(\sigma=30\) to \(\sigma\approx 22\text{--}30\).

\subsection{Sensitivity to the FoG velocity ratio}
\label{sec:results_sensitivity}

\Cref{fig:sensitivity} shows how the combined \(\sigma(f\se)\) from
the calibrated scenario depends on the assumed FoG velocity ratio
\(\rsv\). This parameter simultaneously controls the PFS FoG fiducial
(\(\ctilde^{\PFS}\propto \rsv^4\)) and the asymmetric scale cut
(\(\kmax^{\PFS}\propto \rsv^{-1}\)) in the overlap calibration.

To disentangle these two channels, we show two curves: one with the
default asymmetric \(\kmax\)
(\(\kmax^{\PFS}=\kmax^{\DESI}/\rsv\)), and one with symmetric
\(\kmax^{\PFS}=\kmax^{\DESI}=0.20\iMpch\). The symmetric curve is
flat at \(\sim\!17.5\%\) improvement regardless of \(\rsv\),
demonstrating that the counterterm rescaling alone has no effect on
cosmological constraints at \(\kmax\leq 0.20\iMpch\). The entire
\(\rsv\) dependence comes from the asymmetric scale cut, which
extends the overlap calibration to higher \(k\) and adds up to
\(\sim\!6\) percentage points at \(\rsv=0.5\). At our fiducial
\(\rsv=0.75\), the improvement is 19\%, of which 17.5~pp comes from
the multi-tracer stochastic and bias separation and 1.5~pp from the
asymmetric \(k\)-range extension.

We have also verified that the results are insensitive to the
shared-catalog fraction \(f_{\rm shared}\) between PFS-ELG and
DESI-ELG: sweeping \(f_{\rm shared}\) from 0 to~1, the improvement
on \(\sigma(f\se)\) varies by less than \(\pm 1\) percentage point,
because the different-population cross-spectra
(PFS\(\times\)LRG, PFS\(\times\)QSO)---whose zero cross-shot
noise is guaranteed by construction---dominate the calibration.


%-------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------

We now examine which calibrated parameters drive the improvement,
assess robustness, and discuss the implications for neutrino mass
measurements and the \(S_8\) tension.

\subsection{Which parameters drive the improvement?}

\Cref{tab:importance} decomposes the cosmological improvement by
progressively calibrating subsets of nuisance parameters from the
overlap Fisher while keeping the remainder at their broad priors.
The result is unambiguous---the linear bias \(b_1\se\) is the
dominant driver, providing \(\sim\!70\%\) of the \(f\se\) gain,
\(\sim\!97\%\) of the \(\Mnu\) gain, and \(\sim\!65\%\) of the
\(\Om\) gain.

\centering
\begin{tabular}{lrrr}

Calibrated parameters & \(\Delta\sigma(f\se)\) & \(\Delta\sigma(\Mnu)\) & \(\Delta\sigma(\Om)\) \\
\hline
None (broad)                     & ---       & ---       & ---       \\

All (= calibrated)                & \(-19\%\) & \(-55\%\) & \(-20\%\) \\

\end{tabular}
\caption{Parameter importance decomposition.  Each row uses the

\(b_1\se\) dominates because \(\Mnu\) is nearly perfectly
degenerate with \(b_1\) in the single-tracer Fisher
(correlation \(-0.99\)); the multi-tracer overlap breaks this
degeneracy by constraining \(b_1\se\) from a flat prior to
\(\sigma\approx 0.17\).  Without \(b_1\se\) calibration, the
improvement drops to 4\% on \(f\se\) and \(\Mnu\).}
\label{tab:importance}
\end{table}

The physics behind this is the \(b_1\)--\(f\) degeneracy
intrinsic to single-tracer redshift-space power spectra. \(\Mnu\)
suppresses small-scale power, an effect nearly perfectly
compensated by increasing \(b_1\) (Fisher correlation
\(-0.99\)). With broad priors, \(b_1\se\) carries a flat
(uninformative) prior, so this degeneracy fully propagates into the
marginalized \(\sigma(\Mnu)\). The multi-tracer overlap constrains
\(b_1\se\) to \(\sigma\approx 0.17\), breaking the degeneracy and
reducing \(\sigma(\Mnu)\) by more than a factor of two.
\Cref{fig:fisher_contours} visualizes this mechanism. The broad-prior
ellipse in the \(b_1\se\)--\(\Mnu\) plane is elongated along the
degeneracy direction; the calibrated ellipse collapses it.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{\figurespath/fisher_contours.png}
\caption{Fisher contours (1\(\sigma\) and 2\(\sigma\)) in the
\(b_1\se\)--\(\Mnu\), \(b_1\se\)--\(f\se\), and
\(f\se\)--\(\Mnu\) planes, comparing broad priors (blue)
with calibrated priors (green). The near-perfect
\(b_1\)--\(\Mnu\) degeneracy (left panel) is the dominant

The right panel shows the resulting marginalized cosmological
contour. Fiducial: DESI-ELG at \(z=0.9\), combined over
4 \(z\)-bins in 14,000~deg\(^2\).}
\label{fig:fisher_contours}
\end{figure}

SBPs achieve the same effect through a different
route. Chudaykin et al.~\cite{Chudaykin:2026nls} report that
field-level SBPs shrink the \(b_1\) posterior by 30--40\% for all
DESI tracers---a comparable tightening, but derived from HOD
modeling rather than from data. This parallel validates both
approaches and makes them natural cross-checks of each other.

The dependence on the \(b_1\) prior deserves careful discussion.
Conservative EFT analyses traditionally adopt a flat prior on
\(b_1\)~\cite{Ivanov:2019pdj,dAmico:2019fhj}, deliberately
sacrificing constraining power to avoid prior-driven biases. If one
maintains this convention, the improvement from multi-survey priors
drops to \(\sim\!4\%\) on both \(f\se\) and \(\Mnu\)
(\cref{tab:importance}, ``All except \(b_1\se\)'' row)---a floor
set by the remaining nuisance calibration (\(\Pshot\),
\(b_{\G_2}\), and the counterterms acting jointly).

The headline improvement of 8--54\% therefore requires adopting an
informative \(b_1\) prior---but this is precisely what the
SBP program attempts to do.
Field-level SBPs already shrink \(b_1\) by
30--40\%~\cite{Chudaykin:2026nls}, and power-spectrum-level SBPs
impose HOD-calibrated priors on the full bias vector including
\(b_1\)~\cite{DESI:2025wzd}. The key difference is the source
of the prior information. SBPs derive
\(b_1\) constraints from simulated galaxy--halo connections, while
multi-survey priors derive them directly from cross-spectra between
observed galaxy populations. The latter is model-independent by
construction---it relies on no assumption about the HOD---and thus
provides a cross-check on whether the SBP-calibrated \(b_1\)
values are consistent with what the data themselves imply.


\subsection{Robustness}

Beyond the \(f_{\rm shared}\) insensitivity shown in
\cref{sec:results_sensitivity}, the improvement is stable under
variations of the full-area scale cut. Repeating the combined six-sample forecast at
\(\kmax=0.15\), \(0.20\), and \(0.25\iMpch\), the improvement on
\(\sigma(f\se)\) varies smoothly from 11\% to 8\% to 6\%, and on
\(\sigma(\Mnu)\) from 59\% to 54\% to 49\%. The mild decrease
with increasing \(\kmax\) reflects the growing weight of the
counterterms and \(k\)-dependent stochastic terms, which
the survey overlap cannot constrain, at higher wavenumbers. The
improvement remains significant across the full range of
\(\kmax\) values considered.


\subsection{Complementarity with simulation-based priors and
implications for \(\Mnu\)}

As established in \cref{sec:sbp_comparison}, multi-survey priors
and SBPs constrain the same parameters through independent
mechanisms. This independence makes them a direct cross-check on
the \(S_8\) tension. Chudaykin et
al.~\cite{Chudaykin:2026nls} report that field-level SBPs shift
\(\se\) downward by \(\sim\!2\sigma\) relative to conservative
priors, and whether this shift originates in the HOD modeling or
in new physics is currently unresolved. A future
PFS\(\times\)DESI multi-tracer analysis that reproduces this
shift without HOD input would strengthen the new-physics
interpretation; a null result would point to systematic biases in
the galaxy--halo connection.

The optimal strategy combines both approaches---multi-survey
priors for \(b_1\), \(\Pshot\), and \(b_{\G_2}\); SBPs for the
counterterms and \(k\)-dependent stochasticity that the overlap
volume cannot reach. The 54\% improvement on \(\sigma(\Mnu)\) is
particularly consequential. DESI~DR1 combined with CMB data
already prefers the normal neutrino mass hierarchy over the
inverted hierarchy~\cite{DESI:2024hhd,Chudaykin:2025lww}, and
tightening \(\sigma(\Mnu)\) from galaxy clustering sharpens this
determination at every stage of DESI data releases.

The method generalizes to any pair of overlapping spectroscopic
surveys with different target
selections~\cite{Alarcon:2016bkr}---Euclid\(\,\times\,\)DESI,
Roman\(\,\times\,\)Spec-S5, and
DESI-II\(\,\times\,\)MegaMapper---and joint
J-PAS\(\,\times\,\)PFS forecasts have already explored similar
synergies~\cite{Qin:2025nkk}. Adding the tree-level
bispectrum~\cite{Ivanov:2023qzb,Bakx:2025pop} would activate
the sub-leading counterterm \(c_1\) and break the
\(b_1\)--\(b_2\)--\(b_{\G_2}\) degeneracies through the \(Z_2\)
kernel structure.

The present forecast rests on three assumptions that future works should validate. The Gaussian Fisher approximation captures the leading
information content but not non-Gaussian posterior structure;
an MCMC analysis with the full likelihood would sharpen the
comparison with SBPs. The PFS-ELG EFT fiducials are scaled from
DESI rather than fitted to PFS mocks; validation against upcoming
mock suites---Uchuu-LRG~\cite{FernandezGarcia:2025rso},
Uchuu-ELG~\cite{Vaisakh:inprep}, and Mucho
Uchuu~\cite{Ishiyama:inprep} on the DESI side, and PFS galaxy
mocks~\cite{PFS-CO-mock:inprep} on the PFS side---will anchor
the EFT fiducials and test the transferability assumption.
Finally, the bispectrum, survey window functions, super-sample
covariance, and non-Gaussian covariance are omitted but would
each modify the information
budget~\cite{Ivanov:2023qzb,Bakx:2025pop}.


%-------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------

We have proposed \emph{multi-survey priors}---a data-driven
alternative to simulation-based priors that calibrates EFT nuisance
parameters from the multi-tracer analysis of the PFS--DESI overlap
volume. Our main findings are:

\begin{enumerate}
\item EFT nuisance marginalization is the dominant systematic cost in
  full-shape analyses; conservative priors degrade \(\sigma(f\se)\) by
  a factor of \(\sim\!4.6\) and \(\sigma(\Mnu)\) by
  \(\sim\!10\) relative to perfect nuisance knowledge, across the
  combined six-sample DESI forecast.

\item The multi-tracer overlap calibrates \(b_1\se\) (from a flat
  prior to \(\sigma\approx 0.17\)), \(\Pshot\), and \(b_{\G_2}\se^2\).
  The \(b_1\se\) calibration is the dominant driver---it breaks the
  \(b_1\)--\(f\) degeneracy that limits single-tracer analyses
  (\cref{tab:importance,fig:fisher_contours}).

\item Forecasting all 6 DESI~DR2 tracer samples (LRG1--3,
  ELG1--2, QSO), multi-survey priors improve the combined
  \(\sigma(f\se)\) by 8\% (7--25\% per sample) and
  \(\sigma(\Mnu)\) by 54\% (46--71\% per sample)---achieved
  without any simulation input.

\item Multi-survey priors are not a replacement for SBPs but a
  model-independent cross-check. Both tighten the same parameters
  through different mechanisms; multi-survey priors provide the
  counterterms and stochasticity that SBPs cannot, and vice versa.
  In the context of the \(S_8\) tension, multi-survey priors offer
  an independent handle on whether the SBP-driven \(\se\) shift
  reflects new physics or HOD systematics. The approach
  generalizes to any pair of overlapping spectroscopic
  surveys, including Euclid$\times$DESI,
  Roman$\times$Spec-S5, and DESI-II$\times$MegaMapper.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Covariance matrix}
\label{app:covariance}

The Gaussian multipole covariance for \(N_t\) tracers
follows from Wick-contracting two power spectrum
estimators:
\be
\label{eqn:cov_wick}
\Cov\!\bigl[P_\ell^{XY},\, P_{\ell'}^{WZ}\bigr]\!(k)
= \frac{(2\ell{+}1)(2\ell'{+}1)}{2\,N_{\rm modes}(k)}
  \!\int_{-1}^{1}\!\dd\mu\; L_\ell(\mu)\, L_{\ell'}(\mu)\,
  \bigl[
    P^{XW}\! P^{YZ}
  + P^{XZ}\! P^{YW}
  \bigr],
\ee
where \(N_{\rm modes}(k)=k^2\Delta k\,V/(2\pi^2)\) is the number
of Fourier modes in a shell of width \(\Delta k\), and
\(L_\ell(\mu)\) are the Legendre polynomials. The two terms inside
the brackets are the two Wick pairings of the 4 galaxy fields;
for a single tracer (\(X{=}Y{=}W{=}Z\)) they are identical,
recovering the familiar factor-of-two scaling of the power-spectrum
variance.

The power \(P^{XY}(k,\mu)\) entering the covariance
includes the deterministic signal and all noise contributions:
\be
\label{eqn:ptot}
P^{XY}(k,\mu) =
  P^{XY,\mathrm{det}}(k,\mu)
  + \frac{\delta_{XY}^{\rm K}}{\nbar_X}
  + P_{\rm shot}^{XY}\,,
\ee
where \(P^{XY,\mathrm{det}}\) is the one-loop signal evaluated at the
fiducial parameters, \(\delta_{XY}^{\rm K}/\nbar_X\) is the Poisson
auto-shot noise (present only for auto-spectra), and
\(P_{\rm shot}^{XY}\) is the cross-shot noise from
\cref{eqn:cross_shot}---non-zero only for PFS-ELG\(\times\)DESI-ELG,
and identically zero for cross-spectra between different galaxy
populations.

At each wavenumber, the observable vector has
\(N_{\rm obs}=N_{\rm spec}\times N_\ell\) elements, where
\(N_{\rm spec}=N_t(N_t{+}1)/2\) is the number of auto- and
cross-spectrum pairs and \(N_\ell=3\) for the monopole, quadrupole, and hexadecapole
\(\ell=0,2,4\), respectively. The covariance is therefore an
\(N_{\rm obs}\times N_{\rm obs}\) matrix at each \(k\). The
\(\mu\)-integrals are evaluated with 20-point GL
quadrature. An equivalent approach is the analytic Wigner~3\(j\) decomposition of Rubira \&
Conteddu~\cite{Rubira:2025scu} (their Eq.~2.20), which first decomposes \(P(k,\mu)\) into multipoles \(P_\ell\) and then evaluates the products of Legendre polynomials via
\(L_{\ell_1}L_{\ell_2}=\sum_{\ell_3}(2\ell_3{+}1)
\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}\ell_1 & \ell_2 & \ell_3 \\
0 & 0 & 0\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)^{\!2} L_{\ell_3}\).
The two methods differ when the one-loop power spectrum has \(\mu\)-dependence beyond \(\ell=4\): the GL quadrature integrates the full \(P(k,\mu)\) directly, while the Wigner~3\(j\) formula truncates at the maximum multipole included in the decomposition.
\Cref{fig:cov_validation} shows the convergence. At \(\ell_{\max}=4\) the analytic formula agrees with GL to \(\sim\!10^{-6}\); including the \(\ell=6\) and \(\ell=8\) contributions from the one-loop integrals brings the agreement to \(\sim\!10^{-9}\) and \(\sim\!10^{-11}\), respectively.
The GL quadrature is therefore equivalent to \(\ell_{\max}\to\infty\).
The covariance matrix at every \(k\) additionally passes exact symmetry (\(\|C{-}C^T\|{<}10^{-12}\)), positive-definiteness, and \(\Cov\propto 1/V\) scaling checks.

\centering

\caption{Validation of the Gaussian covariance.
Left: selected covariance elements as a function of \(k\), computed

\subsection{Fisher matrix}
\label{app:fisher_checks}

We verify the assembled Fisher matrix against four checks:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Analytic regression.}
  For a toy model \(P_0(k)=Ak^n+1/\nbar\) with two parameters
  \(\{A,n\}\), the monopole-only Fisher matrix has a closed form.
  Our numerical implementation reproduces it to \({<}\,1\%\).

\item \textbf{Information monotonicity.}
  Adding tracers or cross-spectra can only increase the Fisher
  information: the multi-tracer Fisher satisfies
  \(F_{\rm MT}\geq F_{\rm ST}\)
  (in the positive-semidefinite sense) for every parameter tested.

\item \textbf{Scenario ordering.}
  The marginalized constraints obey
  \(\sigma_{\rm fixed}\leq\sigma_{\rm cal}
  \leq\sigma_{\rm broad}\)
  for all cosmological parameters and redshift bins.

\item \textbf{Cosmic-variance-free limit.}
  In the linear-bias Kaiser model with 2 tracers of different
  bias (\(b_A=1\), \(b_B=2\), \(f=0.8\)) and no counterterms, the
  multi-tracer Fisher for \(P_m\) (marginalized over both biases
  via a Schur complement) recovers the cosmic-variance-free
  floor~\cite{McDonald:2009cvfree}:
  \be
  \label{eqn:ms09_floor}
  \frac{\sigma^2(P_m)}{P_m^2}
  \;\xrightarrow[\nbar\to\infty]{}\;
  \frac{2}{N_{\rm modes}}\,.
  \ee
  \Cref{fig:ms09_convergence} shows the convergence. The
  single-tracer error plateaus above the floor due to the
  \(b\)--\(f\) degeneracy, while the multi-tracer error drops
  monotonically toward it. At
  \(\nbar=10^6\;(\Mpch)^{-3}\) the ratio
  \(\sigma^2_{\rm MT}/\sigma^2_{\rm floor}\) is within 1\% of
  unity, confirming that the covariance-matrix inversion correctly
  cancels the cosmic-variance contribution.
\end{enumerate}

\section{EFT priors from co-evolution}
\label{app:coevolution_priors}

In the baseline analysis, the nonlinear biases \(b_2\) and \(b_{\G_2}\)
are assigned independent Gaussian priors (\(\sigma=5\) for
\(b_2\se^2\) and \(b_{\G_2}\se^2\)). Their fiducial values are set
from the Lazeyras et al.\ \cite{Lazeyras:2015lgp} co-evolution
relations, but the relation itself is not enforced as a prior
constraint.

Mergulh\~{a}o et al.\ \cite{Mergulhao:2021kip,Mergulhao:2023zso} tested
whether imposing co-evolution relations (fixing \(b_2\) and
\(b_{\G_2}\) as functions of \(b_1\)) improves cosmological
constraints in a multi-tracer EFT analysis and found the gain to be
limited at the power-spectrum level (their Appendix~B).

We confirm this finding in our setup. Fixing \(b_2\se^2\) and
\(b_{\G_2}\se^2\) to their co-evolution values (reducing the nuisance space by 2 parameters per tracer) yields the following changes relative to the free-bias baseline with broad priors:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lrrr}
\hline
Parameter & Free \(b_2,b_{\G_2}\) & Fixed \(b_2,b_{\G_2}\) & Change \\
\hline
\(\sigma(f\se)\) & 0.0757 & 0.0731 & \(-3.5\%\) \\
\(\sigma(\Mnu)\) [eV] & 1.075 & 1.056 & \(-1.8\%\) \\
\(\sigma(\Om)\) & 0.0499 & 0.0430 & \(-13.8\%\) \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The improvement on \(\sigma(f\se)\) and \(\sigma(\Mnu)\) is marginal
(\({<}\,4\%\)), confirming that the one-loop power spectrum provides
sufficient \(k\)-dependent information to constrain \(b_2\) and
\(b_{\G_2}\) without theoretical priors from gravitational evolution.
The more notable \(13.8\%\) improvement on \(\sigma(\Om)\) reflects
the \(\Om\)-sensitivity of the \(b_2\) loop integrals, but this is
small compared to the 19--25\% gain from the cross-calibration.
