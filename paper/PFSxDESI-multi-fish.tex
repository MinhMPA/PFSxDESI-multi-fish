\title{Multi-tracers, multi-surveys: data-driven EFT prior calibration from the PFS--DESI overlap}
\author[a,b,c]{Nhat-Minh Nguyen (Nguyễn Nhật Minh)\orcidlink{0000-0002-2542-7233}}
\affiliation[a]{Center for Data-Driven Discovery, Kavli IPMU (WPI), UTIAS, The University of Tokyo, Kashiwa, Chiba 277-8583, Japan}
\affiliation[b]{Kavli IPMU (WPI), UTIAS, The University of Tokyo, 5-1-5 Kashiwanoha, Kashiwa, Chiba 277-8583, Japan}
\affiliation[c]{Institute For Interdisciplinary Research in Science and Education, ICISE, Quy Nhon, 55121, Vietnam}
\emailAdd{nhat.minh.nguyen@ipmu.jp}
\keywords{Large-scale structure of the universe, Power spectrum, Galaxy surveys, Cosmological parameters from LSS}

\abstract{%
Marginalizing over \(\sim\!12\) effective-field-theory (EFT) nuisance parameters per tracer per redshift bin is the dominant cost in full-shape galaxy power
spectrum analyses. Simulation-based methods to obtain informative EFT priors rely on both N-body simulations and halo-occupation-distribution (HOD) model assumptions. We propose \emph{multi-survey priors}: a
data-driven alternative that calibrates EFT nuisance parameters
directly from the multi-tracer analysis of overlapping spectroscopic
surveys, without simulations or HOD models.
%
The method proceeds in two steps. First, a multi-tracer analysis of
the \(\sim\!1{,}200\;\text{deg}^2\) PFS--DESI overlap at
\(0.8<z<1.6\)---using PFS-ELG together with DESI-ELG, DESI-LRG,
and DESI-QSO (up to 4 tracers, 10 cross-spectra per \(z\)-bin)---calibrates
the EFT parameters for DESI samples. Cross-power spectra between different
galaxy populations carry zero stochastic contribution, cleanly
separating shot noise from signal. Second, the calibrated priors are
exported to the analysis of the full \(14{,}000\;\text{deg}^2\) DESI footprint.
The cross-calibration constrains \(\Pshot\) to 11--23\% of its
broad prior and \(b_{G_2}\se^2\) to 13--35\%, improving
\(\sigma(f\se)\) by 26--40\% and \(\sigma(\Mnu)\) by 62--65\%
over the conservative baseline. The improvement is robust to the
shared-catalogue fraction between PFS-ELG and DESI-ELG and
to the assumed FoG velocity ratio. Multi-survey priors
are complementary to simulation-based priors, provide a
model-independent consistency check on HOD assumptions, and
generalize to any pair of overlapping spectroscopic surveys.
}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------

Full-shape analyses of galaxy clustering in the effective field
theory of large-scale structure
(EFTofLSS)~\cite{Baumann:2010tm,Carrasco:2012cv,Senatore:2014eva}
have become a cornerstone of spectroscopic cosmology. Three
independent groups applied the one-loop EFT power spectrum to BOSS
DR12~\cite{Ivanov:2019pdj,dAmico:2019fhj,Chen:2021wdi}, delivering
percent-level constraints on the growth rate, the Hubble constant,
and the matter density within \(\Lambda\)CDM, and subsequently
extending to neutrino masses~\cite{Ivanov:2019hqk}, primordial
non-Gaussianity~\cite{Cabass:2022wjy}, and the galaxy
bispectrum~\cite{Philcox:2021kcw}. Most recently, both the DESI
Collaboration~\cite{DESI:2024fs,DESI:2024cosmo} and independent
reanalyses~\cite{Chudaykin:2025abc} have applied the same framework
to DESI DR1, probing dark energy, modified gravity, and neutrino
masses at the full-shape level. The EFT framework parameterises our
ignorance of small-scale physics through \(\sim\!12\) nuisance
parameters per tracer per redshift
bin~\cite{Chudaykin:2020aoj,Desjacques:2016bnm}: bias coefficients,
counterterms, and stochastic amplitudes (\cref{tab:eft_params}).
Marginalizing over these parameters with conservative Gaussian priors
is the dominant cost in current full-shape
analyses~\cite{Chudaykin:2024abc}.

Tightening the nuisance priors is therefore a direct route to sharper
cosmological constraints. All existing approaches to informative
priors are \emph{simulation-based}: HOD mock catalogues are generated
from N-body simulations, the EFT parameters are measured from the
mocks---either at the power-spectrum level or at the field
level---and the resulting distribution is used as a
prior~\cite{Ivanov:2024sbp,Zhang:2024meth,Ivanov:2024rsd}. Zhang et
al.~\cite{Zhang:2025desi} applied power-spectrum-level priors to
DESI DR1, improving \(\sigma(\se)\) by \(\sim\!23\%\); Chudaykin,
Ivanov \& Philcox~\cite{Chudaykin:2026field} achieved
\(\sim\!50\%\) with field-level priors. These gains are
substantial---but the priors are only as accurate as the HOD model
used to produce them.

Meanwhile, multi-tracer EFT analyses have shown that jointly fitting
multiple galaxy populations breaks parameter degeneracies beyond
what single-tracer analyses can
achieve~\cite{Mergulhao:2022multitracer,Alarcon:2018multitracer}.
Zhao et al.~\cite{Zhao:2024eboss} demonstrated this on eBOSS DR16
data, jointly analysing LRGs and ELGs within a single survey and
improving \(\sigma(\se)\) by 18--27\% depending on the treatment of
cross-stochasticity. However, same-survey multi-tracer analyses
share survey-specific systematics and have non-trivial
cross-stochasticity between overlapping populations.

\emph{Can the EFT nuisance parameters be calibrated directly from
observational data, without HOD modelling?}

We propose \emph{multi-survey priors}---a data-driven alternative to
simulation-based priors that exploits the overlap volume between two
independent spectroscopic surveys. The idea is simple: when PFS and
DESI observe the same patch of sky, their cross-power spectra---especially
between \emph{different} galaxy populations such as
PFS-ELG\(\,\times\,\)DESI-LRG---carry zero stochastic contribution
by construction, since the two catalogues share no galaxies. A
multi-tracer Fisher analysis of the auto- and cross-spectra in the
overlap isolates the stochastic nuisance parameters
model-independently. The calibrated priors are then exported to DESI's
full \(14{,}000\;\text{deg}^2\) footprint, tightening cosmological
constraints without any simulation input.

The \(\sim\!1{,}200\;\text{deg}^2\) overlap of the Subaru Prime Focus
Spectrograph (PFS) ELG survey~\cite{Takada:2014pfs} with three DESI
tracers---ELG, luminous red galaxies (LRG), and quasars
(QSO)~\cite{DESI:2016fdr,DESI:2025forecast}---at \(0.8<z<1.6\)
provides a concrete realization. With up to 4 tracers and 10
cross-spectra per redshift bin, the overlap volume functions as a
calibration laboratory for the EFT parameters that limit the full
DESI analysis. PFS's lower finger-of-god (FoG) contamination further
motivates an asymmetric scale cut
\(\kmax^{\PFS}>\kmax^{\DESI}\)~\cite{Rubira:2025asym}, extending the
usable \(k\)-range in the overlap.

This paper is organised as follows. \Cref{sec:method} describes the
multi-survey prior mechanism. \Cref{sec:surveys} presents the PFS and
DESI survey specifications. \Cref{sec:fisher} details the Fisher
forecast setup. \Cref{sec:results} presents the calibrated priors,
the full-area DESI constraints, the calibration efficiency, and
sensitivity tests. We discuss implications in \cref{sec:discussion}
and conclude in \cref{sec:conclusions}.

\section{Multi-survey priors}
\label{sec:method}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{The marginalization cost}
\label{sec:marg_cost}

The one-loop EFTofLSS model for the galaxy power spectrum multipoles
\(P_\ell(k)\) involves \(\sim\!12\) nuisance parameters per tracer per
redshift bin, summarised in \cref{tab:eft_params}: three bias parameters
(\(b_1\se\), \(b_2\se^2\), \(b_{G_2}\se^2\)) that enter the Fisher
matrix as explicit rows; the cubic tidal bias \(b_{\Gamma_3}\); five
counterterms (\(c_0\), \(c_2\), \(c_4\), \(\ctilde\), \(c_1\)) that
absorb UV sensitivity of the loop integrals; and three stochastic
amplitudes (\(\Pshot\), \(a_0\), \(a_2\)) that parameterise departures
from Poisson shot noise.

In a real MCMC analysis, the counterterms and stochastic amplitudes
enter the model linearly or quadratically and are marginalized
analytically~\cite{Chudaykin:2020aoj}. Their Gaussian priors therefore
propagate directly into the marginalized posterior on cosmological
parameters---broader nuisance priors mean weaker cosmological
constraints, regardless of the sampler used.

The cost of this marginalization is substantial. Zhang et al.\
\cite{Zhang:2025desi} quantified it by comparing DESI DR1 full-shape
results with conservative versus HOD-informed priors: \(\sigma(\se)\)
improved by \(\sim\!23\%\) and \(\sigma(\Om)\) by \(\sim\!4\%\) simply
from tightening the nuisance priors. Chudaykin, Ivanov \& Philcox
\cite{Chudaykin:2026field} pushed further with field-level
simulation-based priors, achieving \(\sim\!50\%\) on \(\sigma(\se)\).
These numbers establish that the nuisance prior is the dominant
bottleneck in current full-shape analyses.


\subsection{Cross-power constraints on nuisance parameters}
\label{sec:cross_power}

The multi-tracer approach exploits the different stochastic properties
of distinct galaxy populations~\cite{Seljak:2009mt,Mergulhao:2022multitracer}.
The auto-power spectrum of tracer \(X\) at multipole \(\ell\) can be
written schematically as
\be
\label{eqn:auto}
P_\ell^{XX}(k)
= P_\ell^{XX,\mathrm{det}}(k;\, b_1^X, b_2^X, \ldots, c_0^X, \ctilde^X)
+ \frac{1}{\nbar_X}\!\left(
  1 + \Pshot^X + a_0^X \frac{k^2}{\knl^2} + \ldots
\right)\!,
\ee
where the first term is the deterministic (signal) contribution from
tree-level and one-loop perturbation theory, and the second is the
stochastic contribution---Poisson shot noise (\(1/\nbar\)) plus
EFT corrections (\(\Pshot\), \(a_0\), \(a_2\)).

The cross-power spectrum between tracers \(X\) and \(Y\) is
\be
\label{eqn:cross}
P_\ell^{XY}(k)
= P_\ell^{XY,\mathrm{det}}(k)
+ P_{\rm shot}^{XY}\,,
\ee
where the cross-stochastic term \(P_{\rm shot}^{XY}\) depends on
whether the two catalogues share galaxies. For \emph{different} galaxy populations---PFS-ELG\(\,\times\,\)%
DESI-LRG or PFS-ELG\(\,\times\,\)DESI-QSO---the catalogues have zero
overlap and the cross-stochastic vanishes exactly:
\(\delta_{XY}^{\rm shared}=0\). These cross-spectra provide a clean,
model-independent measurement of the deterministic signal, free of
any shot-noise contamination.

For \emph{similar} populations---PFS-ELG\(\,\times\,\)DESI-ELG---a
fraction \(f_{\rm shared}\) of DESI-ELGs also appear in the PFS
catalogue (both surveys target [OII] emitters, and PFS goes
\(\sim\!1\;\text{mag}\) deeper in [OII] flux). The cross-shot noise is
\be
\label{eqn:cross_shot}
P_{\rm shot}^{\PFS\times\DESI\text{-ELG}}
= \frac{f_{\rm shared}}{\nbar_{\PFS}}\,.
\ee
This interpolates smoothly between zero (\(f_{\rm shared}=0\),
fully independent) and \(1/\nbar_{\PFS}\) (\(f_{\rm shared}=1\),
all DESI-ELGs also in PFS). We adopt a fiducial
\(f_{\rm shared}=0.5\) and show in \cref{sec:results_fshared}
that the impact on cosmological constraints is negligible
(\(\sigma(f\se)\) varies by \({<}\,\pm 1\) percentage point across
\(f_{\rm shared}\in[0,1]\)).

The key observation is that jointly fitting the auto-spectra
\(P_\ell^{XX}\), \(P_\ell^{YY}\) and the cross-spectrum
\(P_\ell^{XY}\) at each \(k\) over-determines the system: the
deterministic signal is shared (up to bias ratios), while the
stochastic terms enter only the auto-spectra (or are quantifiably
reduced in the cross). The multi-tracer combination therefore
isolates the \emph{constant} shot-noise departure \(\Pshot\)---which
enters the monopole as a \(k\)-independent offset---far more tightly
than any single tracer can. The \(k\)-dependent stochastic terms
\(a_0\) and \(a_2\), by contrast, are degenerate with the leading
counterterms \(c_0\) and \(c_2\) (both scale as \(k^2\)) and
benefit less from the cross-spectrum~\cite{Zhao:2024eboss}. The
one-loop bias structure additionally constrains \(b_{G_2}\) through
its distinct \(\mu\)-dependence in the loop
integrals~\cite{Mergulhao:2022multitracer}.


\subsection{Asymmetric scale cuts}
\label{sec:asymmetric_kmax}

PFS-ELGs reside in lower-mass host halos than DESI-ELGs or DESI-LRGs,
and consequently have lower virial velocity dispersions. In the EFT
framework, this translates into a smaller FoG counterterm:
\(\ctilde^{\PFS} = \ctilde^{\DESI\text{-ELG}} \times \rsv^4\), where
\(\rsv \equiv \sigma_{v,\PFS}/\sigma_{v,\DESI}\).

A smaller \(\ctilde\) means the one-loop EFT expansion converges to
higher \(k\). Since \(\kmax \propto \ctilde^{-1/4} \propto \rsv^{-1}\),
\be
\label{eqn:kmax_asym}
\kmax^{\PFS} = \kmax^{\DESI} \times \rsv^{-1}\,.
\ee
For our fiducial \(\rsv=0.75\), this gives
\(\kmax^{\PFS}\approx 0.27\iMpch\) when \(\kmax^{\DESI}=0.20\iMpch\).

We implement this by splitting the Fisher matrix sum into two
\(k\)-ranges~\cite{Rubira:2025asym}. At
\(k \leq \kmax^{\DESI}\), all \(N_t(N_t{+}1)/2\) spectra contribute
(up to 10 for 4 tracers). At
\(\kmax^{\DESI} < k \leq \kmax^{\PFS}\), the DESI auto-spectra are
excluded and only the PFS auto and the PFS\(\times\)DESI cross-spectra
remain. The total Fisher matrix is
\be
\label{eqn:fisher_asym}
F_{\rm overlap}
= \sum_{k\,\leq\,\kmax^{\DESI}}
  \mathbf{D}^T \mathbf{C}^{-1} \mathbf{D}\,\Delta k
+ \sum_{\kmax^{\DESI}\,<\,k\,\leq\,\kmax^{\PFS}}
  \mathbf{D}_{\rm red}^T \mathbf{C}_{\rm red}^{-1} \mathbf{D}_{\rm red}\,\Delta k\,,
\ee
where \(\mathbf{D}\) is the derivative matrix of the full observable
vector and \(\mathbf{D}_{\rm red}\), \(\mathbf{C}_{\rm red}\) are
the reduced versions excluding the DESI auto-spectrum rows and columns.


\subsection{The prior export mechanism}
\label{sec:prior_export}

From the overlap multi-tracer Fisher~\cite{Tegmark:1997rp}
\(F_{\rm overlap}\) (\cref{eqn:fisher_asym}), we extract calibrated
priors for each DESI nuisance parameter as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Regularise: \(F_{\rm reg} = F_{\rm overlap} + F_{\rm broad}\),
  where \(F_{\rm broad}\) is diagonal with entries \(1/\sigma_{\rm broad}^2\)
  for each nuisance parameter (from \cref{tab:eft_params}) and weak
  cosmological priors (\(\sigma(f\se)_{\rm prior}=10\),
  \(\sigma(\Om)_{\rm prior}=1\), \(\sigma(\Mnu)_{\rm prior}=5\;\text{eV}\)).
\item Invert: \(C = F_{\rm reg}^{-1}\).
\item Extract: \(\sigma_{\rm cal}(\theta_i) = \sqrt{C_{ii}}\) for each
  DESI nuisance parameter index \(i\).
\end{enumerate}

The calibrated \(\sigma_{\rm cal}\) values are then used as Gaussian
priors in the full-area DESI Fisher forecast over
\(14{,}000\;\text{deg}^2\). The lever arm is the volume ratio
\(V_{\rm full}/V_{\rm overlap} \approx 14{,}000/1{,}200 \approx 12\):
the overlap calibrates the nuisance parameters, and the full area
constrains the cosmology with those calibrated priors.


\subsection{Comparison with simulation-based priors}
\label{sec:sbp_comparison}

The cross-calibration proposed here is complementary to---not a
replacement for---simulation-based priors (SBPs).

SBPs~\cite{Ivanov:2024sbp,Zhang:2024meth,Chudaykin:2026field} are
tight: they encode the full joint distribution of EFT parameters from
HOD mocks, capturing correlations that a diagonal Gaussian prior
cannot. However, they are HOD-dependent: the priors are only as
accurate as the galaxy--halo connection model used to generate the
training set.

Cross-calibration is model-independent: it constrains nuisance
parameters directly from the data, using only the EFT framework
itself and the zero-stochastic property of the cross-spectrum. However,
it is volume-limited: the calibration precision scales with the overlap
volume, which is \(\sim\!10\times\) smaller than the full DESI
footprint.

The two approaches can and should be combined. The cross-calibration
provides an independent, data-driven consistency check on HOD-derived
priors: if the SBP-calibrated stochastic parameters differ
significantly from the cross-calibrated values, this would signal a
systematic problem in the HOD model. Conversely, the SBP can tighten
the counterterm parameters that the overlap volume cannot constrain.

The fair comparison for our work is Zhang et al.\
\cite{Zhang:2025desi} (\(\sim\!23\%\) improvement on \(\sigma(\se)\)),
since both analyses operate at the power-spectrum level. The field-level
SBP of Chudaykin et al.\ \cite{Chudaykin:2026field}
(\(\sim\!50\%\) on \(\sigma(\se)\)) represents the ceiling---it breaks
degeneracies inaccessible to two-point statistics and is not directly
comparable.

\section{PFS and DESI ELG samples}
\label{sec:surveys}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------

We consider the overlap of PFS-ELG with three DESI tracer samples in
the shared \(\sim\!1{,}200\;\text{deg}^2\) footprint at \(0.8<z<1.6\).

\paragraph{DESI.}
The Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument~\cite{DESI:2016fdr} covers
\(14{,}000\;\text{deg}^2\) with three galaxy samples relevant to this
analysis. The ELG sample~\cite{DESI:2025forecast} spans
\(0.8<z<1.6\) with peak \(\nbar\sim 10^{-3}\Mpchcubed\) and bias

\paragraph{PFS.}
The Subaru Prime Focus Spectrograph~\cite{Takada:2014pfs} will survey
\(\sim\!1{,}200\;\text{deg}^2\) with an ELG sample spanning

than DESI and achieving \(\nbar\sim 9\times 10^{-4}\Mpchcubed\) at
\(z\sim 1\). The linear bias is \(b_1(z)=0.9+0.4z\).

\subsection{PFS-ELG EFT fiducials}
\label{sec:pfs_fiducials}

Since PFS-ELG mocks with EFT parameter fits do not yet exist, we
construct PFS fiducials by scaling from the DESI-ELG values using
physically motivated prescriptions. The FoG counterterm scales with
the squared virial velocity:
\(\ctilde^{\PFS}=\ctilde^{\DESI\text{-ELG}}\times \rsv^4\), where
\(\rsv\equiv \sigma_{v,\PFS}/\sigma_{v,\DESI}\). We adopt a fiducial
\(\rsv=0.75\) (PFS selects fainter [OII] emitters in lower-mass halos)
and test the range \(\rsv\in[0.5,\,1.0]\) in \cref{sec:results_sensitivity}.
The leading counterterms (\(c_0\), \(c_2\), \(c_4\)) scale with the
bias ratio \(b_1^{\PFS}/b_1^{\DESI}\); the nonlinear biases \(b_2\)
and \(b_{G_2}\) are set from the Lazeyras et al.\
\cite{Lazeyras:2016coevol} co-evolution relations.

For the DESI-LRG and DESI-QSO samples, we set \(\ctilde\) using the
virial velocity dispersions measured by Zhang et al.\
\cite{Zhang:2025desi} (Table~1): \(\sigma_v^{\rm LRG}=6.20\Mpch\),
\(\sigma_v^{\rm QSO}=5.68\Mpch\), \(\sigma_v^{\rm ELG}=3.11\Mpch\).
Since \(\ctilde\propto\sigma_v^4\), this gives
\(\ctilde^{\rm LRG}\approx 6{,}300\;[\Mpch]^4\) and
\(\ctilde^{\rm QSO}\approx 4{,}500\;[\Mpch]^4\)---an order of
magnitude larger than the ELG value, reflecting the massive host halos
of LRGs and the broad-line kinematics of QSOs. The leading counterterm
\(c_2\) scales with the bias ratio \(b_1^X/b_1^{\rm ELG}\).


\subsection{Multi-tracer Fisher matrix}
\label{sec:mt_fisher}

The observables are power spectrum multipoles
\(P_\ell(k)\) for \(\ell\in\{0,2,4\}\)~\cite{Ivanov:2019pdj,dAmico:2019fhj},
computed for each auto- and cross-spectrum pair. At each wavenumber \(k\), the observable vector
has \(3 N_{\rm spec}\) elements (\(N_{\rm spec}=N_t(N_t{+}1)/2\);
up to 30 for 4 tracers).

The Gaussian covariance at each \(k\) is
\be
\Cov\!\left[P_\ell^{XY},\, P_{\ell'}^{WZ}\right]\!(k)
= \frac{(2\ell{+}1)(2\ell'{+}1)}{2 N_{\rm modes}(k)}
  \int_{-1}^{1}\!\dd\mu\; L_\ell(\mu)\, L_{\ell'}(\mu)\,
  \bigl[P_{\rm tot}^{XW} P_{\rm tot}^{YZ}
  + P_{\rm tot}^{XZ} P_{\rm tot}^{YW}\bigr],
\ee
where \(N_{\rm modes}(k)=k^2\Delta k\, V/(2\pi^2)\) and
\(P_{\rm tot}^{XY}(k,\mu)=P^{XY}(k,\mu)+\delta_{XY}/\nbar_X
+P_{\rm shot}^{XY}\), with \(P_{\rm shot}^{XY}\) from
\cref{eqn:cross_shot}.
The \(\mu\)-integrals are evaluated with 20-point Gauss--Legendre
quadrature. The signal \(P^{XY}(k,\mu)\) is computed from the one-loop
EFTofLSS model~\cite{Chudaykin:2020aoj} using the FFTLog
decomposition~\cite{Simonovic:2017mhp} implemented in
\texttt{ps\_1loop\_jax}, ensuring consistency between the covariance
and the derivative evaluation.

Derivatives \(\partial P_\ell/\partial\theta\) with respect to all
nuisance and cosmological parameters are computed via JAX
forward-mode automatic differentiation (\texttt{jax.jacfwd}) through
the full one-loop model. This provides exact derivatives without
step-size tuning; we validate against five-point stencil numerical
derivatives in \cref{app:derivatives}.


\subsection{Analysis scenarios and benchmarks}
\label{sec:scenarios}

The calibrated priors from \cref{sec:prior_export} are applied to the
full-area DESI-ELG Fisher forecast over \(14{,}000\;\text{deg}^2\).
We evaluate four scenarios, distinguished by the nuisance prior source
and the maximum wavenumber \(\kmax\) used in the full-area analysis
(note: this is the full-area \(\kmax\), not the overlap \(\kmax\)
from \cref{sec:asymmetric_kmax}):
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Broad} (\(\kmax=0.20\iMpch\)): conservative Gaussian priors
  from \cref{tab:eft_params}. Baseline.
\item \textbf{Cross-cal} (\(\kmax=0.20\iMpch\)): calibrated priors from
  the overlap Fisher. Same \(\kmax\) as the baseline, isolating the
  effect of tighter nuisance priors.
\item \textbf{Cross-cal-ext} (\(\kmax=0.25\iMpch\)): calibrated priors
  with an extended \(\kmax\). The physical motivation is that tighter
  nuisance priors reduce the projection of counterterm uncertainties
  onto cosmological parameters~\cite{Chudaykin:2024abc}, making the
  analysis less sensitive to EFT truncation errors at higher \(k\).
  This allows a more aggressive scale cut than would be justified
  with broad priors.
\item \textbf{Fixed nuisance} (\(\kmax=0.25\iMpch\)): nuisance parameters
  fixed to fiducial values. Theoretical ceiling---the maximum
  information extractable from the data if nuisance parameters were
  perfectly known.
\end{itemize}
We overlay horizontal reference lines at the published improvement
ratios from the PS-level SBP of Zhang et al.\ \cite{Zhang:2025desi}
(23\% on \(\sigma(\se)\)) and the field-level SBP of Chudaykin et al.\
\cite{Chudaykin:2026field} (50\% on \(\sigma(\se)\)).


\subsection{Transferability assumption}
\label{sec:transferability}

The calibration transfers from overlap to full area if and only if
DESI-ELG in the overlap has the same HOD---and therefore the same
EFT parameters---as DESI-ELG outside. Since DESI's target selection
is spatially uniform by construction, this holds to the extent that
imaging systematics do not induce spatial variation in the ELG
population; DESI's imaging-systematics pipeline is designed to
control this~\cite{DESI:2025forecast}. We state this as an explicit
assumption and note that a violation would manifest as a detectable
mismatch between the calibrated and in-situ posteriors.

\subsection{Calibrated priors}
\label{sec:results_priors}

widths with the conservative broad
priors~\cite{Chudaykin:2025abc,DESI:2024fs} for all 11 DESI-ELG
nuisance parameters. Two parameters stand out. The shot-noise
departure \(\Pshot\) tightens
by nearly an order of magnitude (from \(\sigma=1\) to
\(\sigma\approx 0.1\text{--}0.3\)), reflecting the clean separation
enabled by the zero-stochastic cross-spectrum. The tidal bias
\(b_{G_2}\se^2\) tightens by \(3\text{--}6\times\) (from \(\sigma=5\)

different mechanism from the shot-noise calibration. Together, these

(\(\ctilde^{\rm ELG}=400\) vs \(\ctilde^{\rm LRG}\approx 6{,}300\)).
The remaining counterterms (\(c_0\), \(c_2\), \(c_4\)) show modest
improvement (73--99\%).

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------

The cosmological gains from multi-survey priors are driven by two
parameters through two distinct mechanisms. The shot-noise departure
\(\Pshot\) is constrained by the zero-stochastic property of the
cross-spectrum between different galaxy populations: since
PFS-ELG\(\,\times\,\)DESI-LRG and PFS-ELG\(\,\times\,\)DESI-QSO
share no galaxies, their cross-spectra measure the deterministic
signal free of any shot-noise contamination, pinning down \(\Pshot\)
to 11--23\% of its broad prior. The tidal bias \(b_{G_2}\se^2\) is
constrained by a qualitatively different mechanism---the one-loop
\(\mu\)-structure of the power spectrum, which is sensitive to the
\(b_{G_2}\) operator through loop integrals with distinct angular
dependence. Critically, the \(k\)-dependent stochastic terms \(a_0\)
and \(a_2\) do \emph{not} tighten: they scale as \(k^2\), making
them degenerate with the leading counterterms \(c_0\) and
\(c_2\)~\cite{Chudaykin:2020aoj,Chudaykin:2024abc}. The FoG
counterterm \(\ctilde\) is also poorly constrained, since its
\(k^4\) contribution is sub-percent at \(k\leq 0.25\iMpch\). This
was not the original expectation---PFS's lower FoG was thought to be
the primary advantage---but the dominance of \(\Pshot\) and
\(b_{G_2}\) is in fact a strength, since the improvement does not
depend on uncertain FoG modelling.

A practical subtlety arises for the PFS-ELG\(\,\times\,\)DESI-ELG
cross-spectrum, where both surveys target [OII] emitters and a
fraction \(f_{\rm shared}\sim 0.5\) of DESI-ELGs also appear in the
PFS catalogue, introducing non-zero cross-shot noise
(\cref{eqn:cross_shot}). However, the impact on \(\sigma(f\se)\) is
negligible (\({<}\,\pm 1\) percentage point across
\(f_{\rm shared}\in[0,1]\); \cref{fig:fshared}), because the
different-population cross-spectra (PFS\(\times\)LRG,
PFS\(\times\)QSO)---whose zero cross-shot is guaranteed by
construction---dominate the calibration. This multi-population
structure is a key advantage of using the full DESI tracer suite.

Multi-survey priors and simulation-based
priors~\cite{Ivanov:2024sbp,Zhang:2025desi,Chudaykin:2026field} are
naturally complementary. The former calibrate \(\Pshot\) and
\(b_{G_2}\) from observational data; the latter can tighten the
counterterms and \(k\)-dependent stochastic terms that the overlap
volume cannot constrain. The optimal strategy is to combine both, using the
multi-survey-calibrated values as a data-driven consistency check on
the HOD assumptions underlying the simulation-based
priors---particularly for the shot-noise departure, which depends
sensitively on sub-Poisson corrections from the satellite fraction.
A 26--40\% improvement on \(\sigma(f\se)\) from
\(\sim\!1{,}200\;\text{deg}^2\) of overlap, comparable to the full
PS-level simulation-based prior pipeline~\cite{Zhang:2025desi}, makes
a strong case for maximizing the PFS--DESI overlap area in survey
planning.

The multi-survey prior method generalizes to any pair of overlapping
spectroscopic surveys with different target
selections~\cite{Alarcon:2018multitracer}:
Euclid\(\,\times\,\)DESI, Roman\(\,\times\,\)Spec-S5, and
DESI-II\(\,\times\,\)MegaMapper are natural future applications.
Adding the tree-level bispectrum would further activate the
sub-leading counterterm \(c_1\) (which enters the bispectrum \(Z_1\)
kernel but has zero power-spectrum derivative) and break the
\(b_1\)--\(b_2\)--\(b_{G_2}\) degeneracies through the \(Z_2\)
kernel structure; we defer this to future work.

Several simplifications should be noted. We use a Gaussian Fisher
forecast, which provides a conservative lower bound on the
information content. The PFS-ELG EFT fiducials are scaled from DESI
rather than fitted to PFS mocks (which do not yet exist). We omit
the bispectrum, survey window functions, super-sample covariance,
and non-Gaussian covariance contributions. The comparison with
simulation-based priors uses published improvement ratios rather than
a re-implementation. Each of these simplifications is conservative:
relaxing them would likely strengthen the case for multi-survey
priors.


%-------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------

We have proposed \emph{multi-survey priors}---a data-driven
alternative to simulation-based priors that calibrates EFT nuisance
parameters from the multi-tracer analysis of the PFS--DESI overlap
volume. Our main findings are:

\begin{enumerate}
\item EFT nuisance marginalization is the dominant cost in current
  full-shape analyses: conservative priors degrade \(\sigma(f\se)\) by
  a factor of \(\sim\!8\) relative to perfect nuisance
  knowledge~(\cref{tab:headline}).

\item The cross-power spectrum between independent surveys---especially
  between different galaxy populations (PFS-ELG\(\,\times\,\)DESI-LRG,
  PFS-ELG\(\,\times\,\)DESI-QSO)---carries zero stochastic
  contribution, providing a clean, model-independent handle on the
  shot-noise departure \(\Pshot\). The one-loop bias structure
  additionally constrains \(b_{G_2}\se^2\) through its distinct
  angular dependence. The \(k\)-dependent stochastic terms \(a_0\)
  and \(a_2\) remain degenerate with the counterterms and are not
  significantly tightened.

\item For PFS\(\,\times\,\)DESI with up to 4 tracers and 10
  cross-spectra per redshift bin, the full multi-tracer calibration
  improves \(\sigma(f\se)\) by 26--40\% and \(\sigma(\Mnu)\) by
  62--65\% over conservative priors---substantially exceeding the
  PS-level SBP benchmark of Zhang et al.~\cite{Zhang:2025desi}
  and achieved without any simulation input.

\item The improvement is robust to the assumed FoG velocity ratio
  (\(\rsv\) sensitivity: 34--48\%) and to the
  shared-catalogue fraction between PFS-ELG and DESI-ELG
  (\(\sigma(f\se)\) varies by \({<}\,\pm 1\) percentage point),
  because the dominant mechanism is stochastic separation, not
  counterterm calibration.

\item Multi-survey priors are complementary to simulation-based
  priors: the former calibrate stochastic parameters from data, the
  latter tighten counterterms from simulations. Together they provide
  mutual consistency checks. The approach generalizes to any
  pair of overlapping spectroscopic surveys---including
  Euclid\(\,\times\,\)DESI, Roman\(\,\times\,\)Spec-S5, and
  DESI-II\(\,\times\,\)MegaMapper.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Derivative convergence}
\label{app:derivatives}

All power spectrum derivatives \(\partial P_\ell/\partial\theta\) are
computed via JAX forward-mode automatic differentiation
(\texttt{jax.jacfwd}) through the full one-loop
\texttt{ps\_1loop\_jax} model, avoiding step-size tuning entirely.

We validate against a five-point central-difference stencil for
representative parameters. The median relative error between autodiff
and stencil is \({<}\,10\%\) for \(b_1\se\), \(c_2\), and \(\ctilde\)
across all \(k\)-bins and multipoles tested (\(\ell=0,2\)). The
\(\ctilde\) derivative requires a large stencil step
(\(\Delta\ctilde\sim 50\)) because the \(k^4\) FoG contribution is
small relative to the total power at \(k\leq 0.25\iMpch\); the
autodiff result is insensitive to this issue.


\section{Fisher code verification}
\label{app:fisher_verification}

We verify the Fisher matrix code against three analytic checks:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Analytic regression.} For a toy model
  \(P_0(k)=Ak^n + 1/\nbar\) with two parameters \(\{A,n\}\), the
  monopole-only Fisher matrix has a closed form. Our numerical
  implementation reproduces it to \({<}\,1\%\).

\item \textbf{Information monotonicity.} Adding tracers or cross-spectra
  can only increase the Fisher information: the multi-tracer Fisher
  satisfies \(F_{\rm MT}\geq F_{\rm ST}\) (in the positive-semidefinite
  sense) for every parameter tested.

\item \textbf{Scenario ordering.} The marginalized constraints obey
  \(\sigma_{\rm fixed}\leq \sigma_{\rm xcal\text{-}ext}\leq
  \sigma_{\rm xcal}\leq \sigma_{\rm broad}\) for all cosmological
  parameters and redshift bins.
\end{enumerate}

All 90 unit tests pass in \(\sim\!10\;\text{s}\) on a single CPU core.


\section{Co-evolution prior test}
\label{app:coevolution}

In the baseline analysis, the nonlinear biases \(b_2\) and \(b_{G_2}\)
are assigned independent Gaussian priors (\(\sigma=5\) for
\(b_2\se^2\) and \(b_{G_2}\se^2\)). Their fiducial values are set
from the Lazeyras et al.\ \cite{Lazeyras:2016coevol} co-evolution
relations, but the relation itself is not enforced as a prior
constraint.

Mergulh\~{a}o et al.\ \cite{Mergulhao:2022multitracer} tested
whether imposing co-evolution relations (fixing \(b_2\) and
\(b_{G_2}\) as functions of \(b_1\)) improves cosmological
constraints in a multi-tracer EFT analysis and found the gain to be
limited at the power-spectrum level (their Appendix~B).

We confirm this finding in our setup. Fixing \(b_2\se^2\) and
\(b_{G_2}\se^2\) to their co-evolution values (reducing the nuisance
space by 2 parameters per tracer) yields the following changes
relative to the free-bias baseline with broad priors:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\hline
Parameter & Free \(b_2,b_{G_2}\) & Fixed \(b_2,b_{G_2}\) & Change \\
\hline
\(\sigma(f\se)\) & 0.0757 & 0.0731 & \(-3.5\%\) \\
\(\sigma(\Mnu)\) [eV] & 1.075 & 1.056 & \(-1.8\%\) \\
\(\sigma(\Om)\) & 0.0499 & 0.0430 & \(-13.8\%\) \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The improvement on \(\sigma(f\se)\) and \(\sigma(\Mnu)\) is marginal
(\({<}\,4\%\)), confirming that the one-loop power spectrum provides
sufficient \(k\)-dependent information to constrain \(b_2\) and
\(b_{G_2}\) without theoretical priors from gravitational evolution.
The more notable \(13.8\%\) improvement on \(\sigma(\Om)\) reflects
the \(\Om\)-sensitivity of the \(b_2\) loop integrals, but this is
small compared to the 26--40\% gain from the cross-calibration.
